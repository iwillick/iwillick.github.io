Research overview

Plants have a remarkable ability to survive harsh overwintering conditions while many summer annual crops are susceptible to lethal injury at temperatures just below 0°C. I am fascinated by this diversity and how plants respond to low temperatures. 

Winter wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) crowns were cooled at 2°C h-1. At -4°C, ice aggregated between the leaf sheaths. Tetrazolium chloride vital staining reveals alive (red) or injured (off-white) tissues in cereals cooled to -4°C (no injury), -10°C (injury to vascular tissue), and -20°C (lethal injury to the apical meristem and vascular tissues).

Ice segregation strategies
Protection of meristem tissues in overwintering plant organs is essential for recovery in the Spring. I previously discovered that winter cereal crowns, the critical organ for overwintering survival, develop leaf sheath ice sinks that dehydrate and promote supercooling in apical meristem tissues (Willick et al. 2019). Cold-induced modifications to tissue connected to the shoot apical meristem are believed to inhibit ice propagation (Willick et al. 2018). 

Tissue level ice segregation strategies may play a role in the overwintering survival of other belowground perennating plant organs. I am using switchgrass and perennial monkeyflower rhizomes to assess how intrinsic rates of ice nucleation influence freezing tolerance.

Phenological and molecular induction of acclimation in switchgrass
To ensure winter survival, perennials must be programmed to respond to temperatures favorable for growth and environmental cues that signal seasonal changes.

I aim to characterize phenological and molecular (RNAseq) influence of declining photoperiod and temperature on growth cessation and cold acclimation in three switchgrass populations with contrasting freezing tolerance.

Warming winters shift the type of freezing stress
Increasing winter temperatures and precipitation can change the type of overwintering stress experienced by plants. In Western Canada, winter cereals that historically experience a freezing-dehydration stress may experience more mid-winter thaws that rehydrate and reduce the freezing tolerance of plant tissues (Willick et al. 2021).

Future cultivars need to incorporate enhanced tolerance to multiple overwintering stresses. My current research aims to tease apart the molecular mechanism differentiating these processes using switchgrass populations adapted to Continental (freezing dehydration) and Northeast United States (high-water freezing) environments.
