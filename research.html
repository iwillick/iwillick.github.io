Current Research

Ice Segregation

Protection of meristem tissues in overwintering plant organs is essential for recovery in the Spring. I previously discovered that winter cereal crowns, the critical organ for overwintering survival, develop leaf sheath ice sinks that dehydrate and promote supercooling in apical meristem tissues (Willick et al. 2019 Plant Cell Environ 42: 701-716). Cold-induced modifications to tissue connected to the shoot apical meristem are believed to inhibit ice propagation (Willick et al. 2018 J Exp Bot 69: 1221-1234).

Tissue level ice segregation strategies may play a role in the overwintering survival of other belowground perennating plant organs. With switchgrass and perennial monkeyflower rhizomes, I will assess how intrinsic rates of ice nucleation influence freezing tolerance.

Winter wheat (Triticum aestivum L.) crowns were cooled at 2°C h-1. At -4°C, ice aggregated between the leaf sheaths. Tetrazolium chloride vital staining reveals alive (red) or injured (off-white) tissues in cereals cooled to -4°C (no injury), -10°C (injury to vascular tissue), and -20°C (lethal injury to the apical meristem and vascular tissues).

Acclimation induction in switchgrass

To ensure winter survival, perennials must be programmed to respond to temperatures favorable for growth and environmental cues that signal seasonal changes.

I aim to characterize phenological and molecular (RNAseq) influence of declining photoperiod and temperature on growth cessation and cold acclimation in three switchgrass populations with contrasting freezing tolerance.

Climate change shifts the type of freezing stress

Increasing winter temperatures and precipitation can change the type of overwintering stress experienced by plants. In Western Canada, winter cereals that historically experience a freezing-dehydration stress may experience more mid-winter thaws that rehydrate and reduce the freezing tolerance of plant tissues (Willick et al. 2021 J Agron Crop Sci 207: 88-99).

Future cultivars need to incorporate enhanced tolerance to multiple overwintering stresses. My current research aims to tease apart the molecular mechanism differentiating these processes using switchgrass populations adapted to Continental (freezing dehydration) and Northeast United States (high-water freezing) environments.
